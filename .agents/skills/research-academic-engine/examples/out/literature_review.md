# Literature Review — Persistence of Early Childhood Effects (excerpt, APA 7)

## Scope
This review synthesizes two foundational sources on the persistence and mechanism of early
childhood skill effects, organized by theme rather than by paper.

## Theme 1 — Skill formation is dynamic and self-reinforcing
A skill-formation account holds that early skills raise the productivity of later investment, a
property termed skill complementarity (Cunha & Heckman, 2007). On this view the *timing* of
investment, not only its magnitude, shapes long-run outcomes.

## Theme 2 — Effects appear to persist into adulthood
Consistent with the skill-formation account, evidence from the Perry Preschool Program suggests that
early intervention is associated with higher adult earnings and reduced criminal involvement, with
the largest gains among the most disadvantaged (Heckman et al., 2013).

## Gap
What remains unresolved is the link between program *fidelity* at the field level and these long-run
outcomes; the mediating channel (cognitive vs. schooling) is still contested. This motivates work
that measures fidelity directly and tests it against downstream outcomes.
