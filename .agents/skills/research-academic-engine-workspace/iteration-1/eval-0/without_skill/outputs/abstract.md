# Abstract

These working notes examine the long-run returns to early childhood investment, motivated by a central puzzle: although early childhood interventions reliably produce strong short-run cognitive gains, whether these gains persist into adulthood, and through which channels, remains contested. The notes draw on two foundational contributions. First, Cunha and Heckman (2007) provide the theoretical foundation, arguing that skills are self-productive and dynamically complementary, captured by the maxim that "skills beget skills." Because early investment raises the return to later investment, the timing of investment matters, and early disadvantage compounds over time. Second, Heckman et al. (2013) supply empirical support: the HighScope Perry Preschool Program raised adult earnings and reduced criminal involvement, with the largest returns concentrated among the most disadvantaged participants. Together, the theory and evidence suggest that early investment yields durable, equity-enhancing benefits. The notes close by identifying a research gap: few studies connect field-level program fidelity to long-run outcomes, and the precise mechanisms generating persistence remain unsettled, motivating further inquiry into how implementation quality drives lasting effects.
